# Property rights

Governs rights around ownership, possession, use of land, and use of personal property.

## 0. General

**0.1** All dollar values in this code of law will refer to 2024 dollars adjusted based on CPI.

## 1. Ownership

Full ownership is defined as the right to use, move, exclude the use of, transfer, and dispose of a defined thing in any way, provided that use doesn't infringe on someone else's rights. Property possessed or occupied by a person is assumed to be fully owned by that person unless one of the following exists:

**1.1** A contract limiting the rights for the person possessing the thing.

**1.2** Ownership by someone else immediately prior to taking possession without a contract for transfer of property between them.

* Abandoned property, for example, is not owned immediately prior to someone else later taking possession, but lost or mislaid property is.

**1.3** If the property in question is information other than a trademark and it is not unique, not novel, or obvious prior to creation. 

**1.4** If the property in question is information, is generally known to the public through the prior owner's consensual or negligent means, and it is more than 2 years after it was created. Between 2 and 12 years after the information was created, right to legally exclude the use of the information no longer holds, but the right to a fraction of the value it creates is still held. In the 3rd year after it was created, the owner gets 100% of the profit generated through the use of the information in question, in the 4th year 90%, and so on until the amount reaches 0 and it is public domain.

**1.5** If the property in question is secret information but reasonably comprehensive steps were not taken to keep the information secret, full ownership is maintained except that a person who acquired or exposed the information without being aware of their secret nature may not be held responsible.

**1.6** If the property in question is a trademark but does not have a low likelihood of confusion with other trademarks in its general area and industry of use. 

## 2. Transfer of property

**2.1** Sale, gifting, or inheritance of property follows the rules for contracts with the following addendums:
	**2.1.1** In the case of inheritance, the default is that an estate will be inherited by the person or persons with the closest relationship to the deceased. Multiple people may be chosen for this if their relationship is substantially similar in closeness (for example, children). A will is treated like a contract with a single party (the will writer), and conditions for validity of a contract apply equally to a will. A will created later invalidates any previous will unless otherwise specified or implied in the will.
	**2.1.2** Any gift or partial gift given to someone that is determine to have been taken or derived from wrongful benefits by the gifting party are revokable to the wronged party.

**2.2** Bailment: Property transferred to the custody of another party for a specific purpose (eg storage or repair). There is a default duty to care for the property and return it upon request.

# 3. Contract law

## Contract steps

To be considered a legal contract, it must have gone through the following steps:

**3.1** An **Offer** in the form of definite terms on when the contract is in effect and what effect the contract has. Ambiguities can be resolved using common industry practices, past dealings, legislated defaults, objective evidence of intent, the reasonableness standard, or fairness. The contract must describe an exchange of value (the contractual "consideration"), not including pre-existing obligations. If one party suspects certain contract terms might not be beneficial for the other person, they have a duty to voice that concern, and if the concern is not voiced that is defined as harm at half the cost of what would be estimated if the harm was caused by an active lie. 

* *Justification: half the cost in comparison to fraud is because a lie of omission is less likely to cause harm than an active lie.*

**3.2** An **acceptance** making clear the intention to create legal relations. This can be verbally, in writing, or through conduct that clearly implies acceptance of the contract that would not make sense without the contract terms (eg beginning to fulfill contractual obligations, usage of offered contractual benefits, or continuing business after notification of new contractual terms). Context, language, actions, and negotiation history can affect clearness of acceptance (eg a business context is more likely to be contractual than a social context). 

## Acceptance legality

To be considered a legal contract, the agreeing parties must have the legal ability to make the contract and capacity to understand the contract. See the borough laws for relevant guardianship considerations.

**3.3** A contract must be written in a way that is simple, clear, understandable, and easily readable to all signing parties. Contracts with complex or uncommon language or language unfamiliar to the acceptor have an increased duty to accurately explain for the offeror. The following lists some kinds of things that may contribute to a determination of a confusing contract:

* Cross references that are confusing or cumbersome to track.
* Sentences that are of greater length than necessary.
* Double negatives and exceptions to exceptions.
* Information that is in a confusing or illogical order.
* Words with obsolete meanings or words that differ in their legal meaning from their common ordinary meaning.
* Frequent use of Old English and Middle English words and Latin and French phrases.
* Sections containing information that does not seem to match the section title. 
* Contracts longer than 3000 words that do not contain a table of contents or alphabetical index.
* Conditions and exceptions to the main promise of the agreement not given equal prominence with the main promise.
* Font or type that is difficult to read. 

**3.4** Consent: Agreements must be made without coercion, undue influence, fraud (including fraud by omission), or mistake, ensuring genuine agreement. Consent can come in the form of express consent, implied consent, and hypothetical consent. 

  * **3.4.1** A mistake made by only one party is enforceable unless the counterparty knew about the mistake and took advantage of it or the mistake fundamentally affects the fairness of the contract.
  * **3.4.2** A mistake made by both parties about a fact that material impacts the contract at the time of formation makes the contract voidable unless voiding the contract would cause additional harm.
  * **3.4.3** In the case of determining hypothetical consent, hypothetical consent can exist in situations where a. action is necessary to preserve life, property, or rights,  b. express or implicit consent cannot be obtained in time to preserve those things, c. the action is something reasonable person would view as fair, and d. the acting party had no reason to believe that the counterparty would likely not consent. 

## Other contract considerations

**3.5** Unless explicitly specified otherwise, any contractual transfer can be reversed by either party within 10 minutes while no the counterparty would not incur any significant cost by the reversal or as long as the canceling party pays any significant costs incurred. 

# Disputes

## 4. Types of harm and equivalences of harm

**4.1** **Harm to or loss of person or property**. This includes temporary loss or devaluation.
	**4.1.1**  Loss of utility value or market value of the property. This includes costs to remedy the harm done when the cost of the remedy is less than the harm that would incur if a remedy were not made (for example medical and professional services required to treat an injury).
	**4.1.2**  Sentimental value of the property. The following guide should be used to determine the compensation rate for sentimental value:
			a. Individually significant - Something with significance to an individual because of the origin or associated event: $100 - $1000.
			b. Family or group significance - Something with historical family or group significance that would be significant not only to the plaintiff but to people who do not yet hold the item to have sentimental significance (including unborn descendants): $1000-$50,000 
			c. Broader significance - An item that has significance beyond a specific group, perhaps a national treasure or a famous artifact:  $10,000 - $1 million

​	**4.1.3**  Failure to fulfill contractual obligations including anticipatory failure (where the failing party indicates that they will not be able to fulfill their obligations).

**4.2** **Harm of experience**. 

​	**4.2.1**  Emotional harm, mental harm, pain, and suffering in the following categories
​		a. Stress or pain while enduring the immediate harm done or while defending from harm.
​		b. Stress or pain while recovering from the harm done. 
​		c. Long term stress or pain resulting from the experience. 

​	**4.2.2**  For individualized harm of experience, the following guide should be used to determine the compensation rate for each minute this kind of harm is experienced. 

​		a. Mild: 0.5¢ to 1¢ per minute. 
​		b. Moderate: 1¢ to 5¢ per minute. 
​		c. Strong: 5¢ to 10¢ per minute. 
​		d. Severe: 10¢ to 50¢ per minute. 
​		e. Unbearable: 50¢ to $1 per minute. 

**4.3** **Nuisances**. 

Nuisances are harm as a result of distracting, unpleasant, or disrupting matter or energy. Nuisances have statutory equivalences of harm following the **Nuisance harm formula** defined as  `P*S*(10+L)` where

* `P` is the estimated number of people affected.
* `L` is the length of time in seconds that the nuisance that was produced.
* `S` is the "scale" of the nuisance beyond the minimum. This is a value defined by the law for the specific type of instance. The standard for this should generally be related to nuisances that cause a deviation from the natural baseline level for the area (ie before/without human settlement).
* The result is a number of 2023 cents.
* Modifiers:
  * A nuisance that is commonly difficult to acclimate to or is distracting is counted as triple harm.
    * Examples: Beeping sound, blinking light, sound that frequently changes in volume or pitch, light that frequently changes in color or brightness, etc.
  * A "nuisance" that is commonly considered to be pleasant and calming by a vast majority of people is counted as 1/10th harm. A "nuisance" that is commonly considered to be enjoyable but not calming by a vast majority of people is counted as 1/3rd harm. It is recognized that "nuisances" that might be pleasant during the day are not pleasant during sleeping hours. 
  * A nuisance that happens during sleeping hours of 10pm and 8am and affects a residence is counted as 5-times harm.
    * *Justification: a nuisance at night that can wake people up causes more harm than during the day. And once a nuisance has disturbed someone further noise is still harmful but not as much as the start of the disturbance.*
  * Note that modified scale of the nuisance in zones officially designated "commercial" or "industrial" must not to be misinterpreted to allow a violation of rights on property in adjacent zones. If noise is created in one of these zones but is received in a different zone, the law for receiving zone is what is applied.
* *Justification: `10+L` assumes a distraction takes you out of your thought process for about 10 seconds.*

​	**4.3.1**  The measurements of a nuisance can be made in the location of the affected person or in a location the affected person avoids because of the nuisance. On private property, only the owner or resident of the property has a right to suit, and they can claim harm done for any guest on their property.

**4.4** **Opportunity cost**: This includes the value of time taken up, opportunities missed during that time, or other opportunities past or future that were or would be lost as a result of the offender's actions. This includes losses of opportunity caused by reputational harm, ie information (whether false, misleading, or inappropriately divulged) that damages to someone's reputation. 
​	**4.3.1**  Value of time taken. Harm that comes from forcing someone to use their time for something they don't want to spend time doing. This includes any time taken as a result of assault, trespass, obstruction, or confinement that takes up a person's time. The non-monetary value of time taken is statutorily set at 30¢ per minute. 
​	**4.3.2**  Specific opportunities that could not be take advantage of because of the harm done as well as general expected opportunities likely lost, including opportunities for income or enjoyment (eg because of reputational harm, bodily damage, or stolen property). The greatest set of opportunities that realistically could have been taken advantage of may be used. This includes any sunk costs related to preparing for such missed opportunities that were incurred before the harm took place.
​	**4.3.3**  General expected opportunities likely lost (eg because of reputational harm, taken or devalued property, disability, etc).
​			**4.3.3.1** For tasks and actions with a monetary value to the claimant that are made more difficult because of the harm done, the following guide should be used to determine a monetary measure of harm done:

​			a. Mild difficulty: 10% of the monetary value of the action.
​			b. Moderate difficulty: 20% of the monetary value of the action.
​			c. Severe difficulty: 50% of the monetary value of the action.
​			d. Inability: 100% of the monetary value of the action.

​			**4.3.3.2** For tasks and actions with no direct monetary value that are made more difficult because of the harm done, including things like loss of consortium (ie damage to a relationship) and loss of social trust, the following guide should be used to determine a monetary measure of harm done per minute the difficulty affected or is expected to affect the person harmed:

​			a. Mild difficulty: 0.5¢ to 1¢ per minute.
​			b. Moderate difficulty: 1¢ to 5¢ per minute.
​			c. Severe difficulty: 5¢ to 20¢ per minute.
​			d. Inability: 20¢ to $1 per minute. 

#### Some examples of torts broken down into the above types of harm

1. Trespass: Loss of time in dealing with whoever was trespassing, any vigilance needed to check for other mischief the trespasser might have done, and mental harm from stress dealing with the situation.
2. Trespass to chattels: Harm to or loss of property
3. Conversion to chattels: Harm to or loss of property
4. Defamation: Reputational harm and mental harm.
5. Intrusion upon seclusion and public disclosure of private facts: Reputational harm and mental harm.
6. Appropriation (eg identity theft): reputational harm, emotional or mental harm, and loss of time.

## 5. Right to suit

**5.1** A party directly harmed by an action has a right to sue about that harm.

**5.2** A party harmed indirectly by an action via contractual obligations to the person directly harmed or indirectly harmed in this way (ie via contractual obligation) has a right to sue about the harm to themselves via harm to someone else.

**5.3** A party has a right to sue about harm if any party with the right to sue about that harm has given contractual authorization to that party. 

**5.4** A party has a right to sue about harm of known parties without their explicit consent if A. more than 20 separate parties were likely harmed and B. they have all been notified and given an opportunity to opt out of the lawsuit. The right to sue begins 2 months after notice has been given to all known parties.

**5.5** A party has a right to sue about harm of unknown parties if A. more than 20 separate parties were likely harmed and B. either reasonable attempt has been made to locate them without success or clear and convincing evidence can be shown that the cost of locating the parties in question would exceed 5% of the harm done to the unknown harmed parties.

**5.6** A party has a right to sue about harm to someone who is not or is no longer capable of bringing suit themselves and who does not have a capable guardian who could bring suit. 

**5.7** A person witness to or with evidence of harm done to someone by one of their guardians. 

## 6. Trial

**6.1** When suit is initiated by a plaintiff, after the defendant has acknowledged the case and this legal code has been chosen, the plaintiff and defendant can discuss a mutually agreeable private court company to handle the dispute. If they cannot agree or one of the parties refuses to discuss, they must use the default borough court.

**6.2** In cases where the claimed harm exceeds $25,000, the defendant has the right to a jury trial if they so choose. Compensation for the jury will be paid for by the losing party. Remedies exceeding a cost of $25,000 may not be ordered if a jury trial option was not offered to the defendant.

**6.3** Court proceedings using this legal code will be conducted using statutory law. Common law is not used. Precedent may be used in argument, but is not law. Law and contract may be interpreted by any defendant in any way a reasonable person might literally interpret it.  

**6.4** Evidence presented:
​	**6.4.1**  must be relevant to the case.
​	**6.4.2**  must be admissible. Hearsay and character evidence is not admissible without exceptional reason. Any evidence collected the release of which in court would constitute harm must have the value of that harm adjudicated prior to its use in court, and the plaintiff using such evidence must be held responsible for that harm regardless of the outcome of the case in which that evidence is used. **How is equivalences of harm determined for things like private in-confidence communication (eg between attorney, doctor, spouse, etc)? **
​	**6.4.3**  Witnesses must be competent and credible to testify. 
​	**6.4.4**  The best available evidence must be used (eg an original document vs copies). 

**6.5** For any type of harm done being claimed, it is determined whether the defendant is at fault or not. Intent or negligence are the two ways one can be at fault. See the section *Defenses for a claim of fault*.

**6.6** For any of the harms the defendant is at fault of, the amount of harm is estimated as a dollar value. Harm done is either A. the lesser of the cost of fully correcting the harm done, the value a person lost because of the harm, or some combination of the two, or B. if no harm was done, the likelihood that harm would have been caused multiplied by the average expected harm.

**6.7** The likelihood of getting caught and convicted is estimated for each harm done. *Things like premeditation, preparation, and intent can factor into the likelihood of getting caught. Someone who premeditated a crime is less likely to be caught than someone who did it in a moment of passion.*

**6.8** The remedies are determined based on the amount of harm that has been estimated having been done and their likelihoods of getting caught. The 'judgement damages' are defined as harm done divided by the likelihood of getting caught and receiving judgement. There must be at least a preponderance of evidence that the net cost to the indicted defendant does not exceed the 'judgement damages'. There must be beyond a reasonable doubt that the net cost to the offender of any confinement ordered is not greater than 'judgement damages' multiplied by the fraction of harm being remedied by confinement.

Possible remedies:
	**6.8.1** Contract performance: An order to fulfill contractual obligations. This may not be ordered if the counter-party did not at least partially fulfill the contract.
	**6.8.2** Contract rescission, in the case the contract is fully voidable.
	**6.8.3** Contract reformation, in the case the contract is partially voidable.
	**6.8.4** Damages: Money paid to the harmed party to compensate them for the harm done. Any damages for expected future manifestation of harm (eg long term health effects from an injury) will be put in a trust and periodically evaluated for what actual harm has manifested and from that information determine whether paid out damages should be adjusted.
	**6.8.5** Fines: Money paid to the adjudicators, prosecutors, and investigators or returned to the community.
	**6.8.6** Property restitution: Return of taken property.
	**6.8.7** Confinement. Prison, rehabilitation facility, at home, etc. This remedy may only be ordered if either other remedies are not feasible or if the defendant presents a high likelihood of causing additional harm if left unconfined. 
	**6.8.8** Optional remedies. These are remedies that may only be offered as an alternative option to other types of remedies outside of this section which will be the default if the defendant rejects the offer. 
		**6.8.8.1** Community service.
		**6.8.8.2** Probation: Requirements and limits placed on the offender for a specified period of time.

#### 6.9 Defenses for a claim of fault

**6.9.1** **Tender of performance**: When one party offers to fulfill their contractual obligations but the counter party prevents them from doing so, the obligation is generally considered fulfilled (they are discharged of their duty) and the counter party must still fulfill their contractual obligations.

**6.9.2** **Partial performance**: If the harmed party also only partially fulfilled their contractual obligations, the defendant may only be held responsible for the net harm done to the counter party after subtracting the harm that the defendant sustained from the counter party.

**6.9.3** **Frustration of purpose**. When unforeseeable events make contractual obligations impossible or overly burdensome to fulfill (eg death of a party, destruction of the essential subject matter, etc). If the harmed party can easily recover benefits they conferred on the defendant, this is the preferred remedy for this. For benefits that cannot be easily recovered, a court may order damages the defendant pays the harmed party for the benefits received that were not compensated by the unfulfilled contractual obligations.

**6.9.4** **Bankruptcy** may be used to discharge claimed contractual obligations of a monetary nature or interchangeable commodity nature.

**6.9.5** **Defense of self, others, or property**. Harm done to an attacker to defend one's person or property or the person or property of another from imminent or ongoing threat from that attacker, is not the fault of the defender as long as the harm done is not greater than the cost of punishment that would be ordered for the harm a reasonable person would have thought likely to result from the attack or from counter defense by the attacker if the defender used a less harmful method of defense. The defender is at fault for harm done in excess of that amount. 

* Warning must be given to the attacker when possible without compromising the ability of the defender to defend.
* Incapacitating force may only be used as a last resort when all lesser means have failed or cannot be reasonable used.
* A person may use deadly force to defend their person against imminent deadly force or great bodily injury, someone else against the same. Deadly force may only be used as a last resort when all lesser means have failed or cannot be reasonable used.

**6.9.6** **Pursuit and detainment** of someone who has caused harm. The standards are the same for defense of self, others, or property above. 

**6.9.7** **Necessity**. If the defendant prevented greater harm to the plaintiff via the harm done in question, the defendant is not at fault. If the defendant prevented greater harm to someone other than the plaintiff, then the defendant is responsible for 1/2 of the harm done, and the benefiting party is responsible for 1/2 as well.

**6.9.8** **Duress**: If a person was credibly threatened by someone and coerced into causing a 3rd party harm, and the threatening party is at fault for any harm done up to what might reasonably have been expected that they might do to the coerced party if the coerced party did not cooperate, and the coerced party is responsible for any harm done to the 3rd party greater above that amount. *For example, if the threatening party threatened the coerced party with $100 of damage and the coerced party harmed the 3rd party to the level of $150 of damage, then the threatening party is responsible for 2/3rds of harm and the coerced party is responsible for 1/3 of the harm.*

**6.9.9** **Caveat Emptor**: A contractual obligation given that causes harm to the receiver, this is not the fault of the party fulfilling the contractual obligation unless the obligation was fulfilled in a way not made clear to the receiving party nor in a way the receiving party could reasonably easily made themselves aware of. Considerations for specific types of contractual obligations:
​	**6.9.9.1** Products or services: The receiver has a default right to expect the product or service to be safe unless otherwise contractually specified or the relevant safety concern is commonly known.
​	**6.9.9.2** Leased space: The renter has a default right to expect the property to be safe, habitable and for the property under normal use to be maintained in its condition when the lease contract was signed with prompt repairs for anything other than damage that is the fault of the renter. The renter also has a default right to quiet enjoyment of the property. By default, 2 months notice must be given for eviction. 
​	**6.9.9.3** Occupational space: A person employed and directed to work in a location has a default right to expect the location to be reasonably safe.
​	**6.9.9.4** Agent: An agent who is given rights to act on another's behalf has the default responsibility to act in the principal's best interest with reasonable skill and diligence, and to inform the principle of relevant events and facts as well as on all actions taken on their behalf. The principal has a responsibility to reimburse expenses the agent incurs while acting as the principal's agent. In the case an agent has done an unauthorized action on the principal's behalf, if the principal explicitly or implicitly accepts these actions, they become retroactively authorized.
​	**6.9.9.5** Information: If a person communicates incorrect or substantially misleading information to a counterparty that causes harm, it is defensible if any of the following are true:
​		a. the supplier of the information was not aware the information was false or misleading and was not representing the information as a certainty or was representing the information as an opinion,
​		b. a reasonable person would not have believed the information to be certainly true or would not have acted on the information without verifying it.
​	**6.9.9.6** Guardianship considerations: It is not defensible if fulfilling the contractual obligation to a ward without permission from a guardian that are likely to have substantial negative consequences for the dependent that the dependent is unlikely to be aware of, able to handle, and avoid. This includes the sale of mind altering substance and other products or services with a high propensity for addiction of the receiver or user.

**6.9.10** **Presumption of autonomy**: A person interacting with someone who looks (to a reasonable observer) 20 years old or older or presents convincing evidence that they have autonomy in their relevant decisions, the defendant is not responsible for harm that results, but a court may order material transfers from the dependent be returned if it doesn't result in a substantial net loss to the defendant. 

**Time limitations**: 
​	**6.9.11** Normal lawsuits must be brought within 3 years of the harm being discovered by the harmed party,
​	**6.9.12** Normal lawsuits brought after 10 years of the harm being done have a reduction in allowable total cost imposed on the defendant by 10% per year (subtractive, ie 90% then 80% then 70% etc), regardless of the date of discovery.
​	**6.9.13** For normal lawsuits where the harmed party was killed, hidden, or otherwise forcibly silenced, the above limitations only start when the harmed party ceases to be forcibly silenced. 
​	**6.9.14** Lawsuits for harm to parties that did not all give explicit consent to the suit must be brought within 5 years of harm being widely known.

**Intellectual property:** 

​	**6.9.15** If secret information was derived by the defendant from non-secret information, no fault can be claimed against the defendant for use of that derived information.

​	**6.9.16** If less than 10% of appeal of a derivative work is derivative of the source work, the source author can claim no harm done.

​	**6.9.17** If less than 10% of the source work is used in the derivative work is derivative of the source work, the source author can claim no harm done.

## 7. Use of force

* Policing
  * **7.1** A person can detain a suspect for no longer than 15 minutes if the detainer has probable cause of harm amounting to damage of greater than $50 (2023) if the suspect refuses to produce identification and refuses to allow a picture to be taken of them or refuses to offer a name and address. The detainer can search the suspect's person in this time, but must return all items after they are released. 
  * **7.2** A person can take a policing training class to obtain a license for elevated policing privileges. 
    * The training taken must cover the following topics:
      * Mediation
      * De-escalation
      * Crisis intervention
      * Legal rights including use of force (see *Defense of self, others, or property*)
      * Best practice strategy and procedure
      * Appropriate defense
      * Appropriate apprehension and arrest
      * Appropriate search and seizure
      * Weapons training
      * Defensive and offensive tactics including techniques to limit the damage done in a fight
      * First aid and CPR
      * Adversarial driving
      * Community collaboration
      * Negotiation strategies
      * Communication strategies
      * Human psychology
      * Mental techniques for preparing for a stressful situation, maintaining focus, and destressing afterward.
    * The privileges successfully passing training gives are:
      * Detaining and searching a person with only reasonable suspicion.
      * Making an arrest with the same pre-conditions as detainment for a person who has not taken policing training. In addition, there must be probable cause to suspect the person is a flight risk or that the likelihood of repeat offense is high. * Holding
  * **7.3** An establishment must be licensed to forcibly hold people. To obtain and maintain a license, a borough court must inspect the facility and its records once per year. The holding facility has a duty to retain and maintain all records about the past 4 years. 
  * **7.4** All those who work on site must have taken policing training. 
  * **7.5** A holding facility is responsible for half the damage caused by a person who escapes early, and 10% of the damage one person forcibly held does to another unless that damage was done intentionally for the purpose of causing that liability the holding facility. 
* **7.6** When estimating damage for policing or in court, the following must be included in the estimation:
  * Actual damage. Defined as the market or legal value of the things damaged. 
  * Attempted damage. Actual damage that would have happened if they succeeded, multiplied by some fraction related to the credibility of the attempt. 
    * *Example: Half hearted attempts might multiply the damage by 1/20th, while serious premeditated attempts might multiply by 1/2.*
  * Risked damage. This is the average actual damage likely to have happened in an accident multiplied by some fraction related to the increased likelihood of that accident that the behavior of the person in question caused. The estimation must not only hold for an average person, but must reasonably hold for the person in question.
  * Fractional damage. Damage done as a group, weighted by the individual's estimated contribution to the damage done.
