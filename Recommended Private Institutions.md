# Private Institutions

* Private police patrol, investigators, and enforcers.

* Private holding facilities (prisons and jail).
  
  * Those being held must be given some choice as to where they are held, so as to foster market competition. 
  
* Private courts.

* Private public space maintainers (for parks, public squares, public ways, etc).

* Private utility operators. Eg electricity, water, sewer, internet, garbage, etc.

* Election betting pools.

* Insurance monopolexes

  * Prison sentence insurance - Insurance that compensates the borough for the costs of imprisoning offenders. 
  * Personal property rights violation insurance - Insurance that compensates a person for harm done to them that cannot be recovered from the offender. This should include any punitive damages owed.
  * Fire insurance
  * Flood insurance
  * Emergency health insurance - Insurance that incentivizes public health and safety.
  * Littering and cleanliness insurance - This is insurance that pays out depending on how dirty a covered location is, for the purpose of incentivizing programs for preventing littering and promoting cleanliness. 

# 1. Private court services

## Dispute Procedure 

### 1. Pre-trial

**1.1** Plaintiff presents a claim of harm done to themselves or their representees and the court decides if there is prima facie evidence of the claim. If not, the case is dismissed. The judge who evaluates the evidence in this step may not be employed in the remainder of the trial.

​	*Justification: The judge should not be used for the rest of the trial because their view of the case is colored by a one sided view of the evidence.*

**1.2** If both parties agree to an available date, that date is selected. If either party requests alternative date selection, both parties will be asked to approve 10 possible dates within the next 30 days, and if there is a coinciding date, the court will select one. If there is no coinciding date, the court will select a date at their discretion. If a date within the next 30 days does not work for either party and their counterparty wants it to be resolved within that 30 days, the court will allow a delay with evidence of the conflict and a fee of $30 per day of delay.

Between date selection and the court date, the following things must take place:

​	**1.3** The defendant is asked what facts of the claim they would like to dispute, if any. A time limit of one week from the selection of the date is given for this.

​	**1.4** Adjudicator selection: Each party chooses a judge, and those two judges chooses a third. If a jury is required, the judges will select the jury in a process where each juror is selected by a 2/3rds vote amongst the judges. 

​	**1.5** Discovery: each side introduces evidence they want to present. This must be done before the selected court date. Plaintiff discovery is only viewable by the defendant after he has decided if there are any facts of the claim they would like to dispute, or the time has expired. Any compulsion of the production of evidence or appearance of witnesses must obtain a warrant from the borough or city court.

​	**1.6** Optional motions (dismiss, suppress evidence, protective order, limine, continuance). 

### 2. Trial

**2.1** In each of the four trial stages, the defendant and plaintiff present their cases and then the judges must agree with a vote, and if a jury is being used the jury must also agree with a vote.  Trial stages requiring a 'preponderance of evidence' require 2/3rds votes and trial stages requiring 'beyond a reasonable doubt' require unanimous votes.  

**2.2** Trial stages:

​	**2.2.1** Determination of fault.

​	**2.2.2** Likelihood of getting caught and convicted. 

​	**2.2.3** Quantification of harm.

​	**2.2.4** Remedies.

## 3. Appeals procedure

**3.1** The losing party can file for appeal within 30 days. This is the same process as the Dispute Procedure except the three judges from the previous trial are used and those three judges select two more judges. Only one appeal at this level is permitted, additional appeals may be possible by appealing to borough or city court.
