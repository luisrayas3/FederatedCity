# City government

## Summary

*This summary is non-normative*

* A government based on economic principles. 
* Federal design with a city government and many borough governments within it.
* Strict limitations on borough size and shape, to ensure that "voting with your feet" is easy. 
* Right of secession with certain requirements.
* City government limited to designating a minimal network of roads and paths, designating nature-space, and settling disputes with borough and city government. 
* Borough government limited to defining property rights, promoting efficient competition, taxing or subsidizing externalities, income tax only if necessary, resolving disputes that can't be resolved privately, very limited licensure and information disclosure requirements, UBI as the only welfare option, limited externality-based zoning, and designation of further travel ways, parks, and nature space.
* Two legislative bodies that both must approve changes to the law: council and the people (via local committees)
* Voting weighted by population vs owned land value vs owned land area must also both pass.
* Limited amount of action council can take per year (in terms of length of time and number of laws) so that the people don't need to spend much of their lives thinking about and considering fighting / supporting legal changes. Also inherently limits the power of politicians and the government.
* Supermajority requirement for laws (60%)
* Statutory law.
* Consequentialist outcome-based law. The law may not reward or punish specific methods and means, and must only prescribe outcomes. 
* Right to choose private law
* Use of force is equal to all but limited.
* Military and national defense is assumed to be taken care of by a higher government. 

## City Government Structure and Powers

The city may not use or delegate powers that aren't explicitly enumerated in this constitution.

### Legislative branch

There are two legislative bodies: the council and local committees.

**1. Council:**

* **1.A** Three council members are elected in a proportional vote from each borough, with each receiving a vote-weight in the council according to the fraction of the vote they received.
* **1.B** Council votes are counted using three different borough weighting methods:
  * Population weighting: The total vote weight of each triple of council members for a borough shall be proportional to the resident population of the borough. 
  * Value weighting: The total vote weight of each triple of council members for a borough shall be proportional to the total land value of the borough.
  * Land area weighting: The total vote weight of each triple of council members for a borough shall be proportional to the total land area of the borough.
    * *Justification: City vs rural has for millennia been a contentious struggle, and ensuring that there is a weighting that favors less populated areas prevents the city government from disadvantaging rural areas in favor of dense areas, which allows the city structure to be functional with a far larger jurisdiction than it otherwise would.*
* **1.C** The council can only take official actions 2 months out of the year. 
  * *Justification: This ensures that council members aren't full time politicians, but instead must have non-political occupations most of the year.*

**2. Local committees:**

* **2.A** Local committees are made up of residents and land owners. Each boroughs define how local committees operates in their jurisdiction.
* **2.B** When all committees have voted on a particular proposal, if the total vote among all the local committees in the city passes, proposal passes for the local committees.
* **2.C** Like council members, votes are weighted in three different ways:
  * Weighting methods:
    * Resident weighting: Every resident voter gets equal weight, non-residents get 0 weight. One must live in the borough for greater than 50% of the year to be considered a resident. 
    * Land value weighting: Every voter has a vote weight equal to the **value** of land they own in the borough. 
    * Land area weighting: Every voter has a vote weight equal to the **area** of land they own in the borough. 

**3. Council and local committee actions:**

* **3.A** An "X% passing vote" in this section means that both legislative bodies pass the proposal with a vote of X% using all three weighting methods. An "X% limiting vote" in this section means that either legislative body pass the proposal with a vote of X% using any one of the three weighting methods. 
  * *Justification: Both council members and citizens must vote on things because elected leaders are in a position to more fully investigate and voice their leadership in a way most citizens can't, elected leaders can be held accountable for constitutional violations in a way citizens can't, but a vote of the citizens ensures that if politicians lie about their preferences, those lies can't translate into adverse policy.*
  * *Justification: The "limiting vote" only requires one legislative body and only one of the three weighting methods because it is the inverse of a passing vote and will only be used to limit the government.*
* **3.B** A law can be created with a 60% passing vote. A law that only contains government limitations can be repealed with a 60% passing vote. Up to 2000 words of law can be proposed per year by each legislative body.
  * *Justification: This limits how much work the people need to do to evaluate new proposed laws, reducing information and voting costs to a hopefully manageable level.*
* **3.C** A law that only contains government limitations can be created with a 45% limiting vote. A law that does not contain government limitations can be repealed with a 45% limiting vote. Up to 4000 words of law can be proposed for repeal per year. This includes bills to amend a law which only includes striking line items.
  * *Justification: When voting on a law that limits the people, any 40% vote with any weighting from either judicial body can result in the vote failing. Therefore, repeal of such laws should be possible with a similar situation. 45% is used here instead of 40% so that a 5% swing is necessary to change the law, which increases the stability of laws.*
* **3.D** A *city limitation* or *borough limitation* can be created with at least a 50% passing vote. A city or borough limitation may only limit the city's and/or borough's powers and may not place further limits on the people. A city limitation can only be repealed, partially or fully, with a passing vote greater than the minimum of the 6 vote counts achieved (population vs value vs area weighted and council vs local committees), up to a maximum of 75%. Up to 2000 words of city limitation or borough limitation can be proposed per year by each legislative body.
  * *Justification: This gives the legislature a way to enact limitations to government power that are variably "sticky" and more flexible / less intrusive than a constitutional amendment.*
* **3.E** A constitutional amendment can be created with a 75% passing vote by local committees only. 
  * *Justification: Politicians are limited by the constitution and so have a conflict of interest. Therefore politicians should not be able to block constitutional changes.*
* **3.F** The local committees can reduce or forgive a sentence for constitutional violation with a 75% passing vote.
* **3.G** Any proposed change to the law or constitution must be first proposed and made public, and to enact the change, a passing vote must happen after 3 months if it being publicly proposed.
  * *Justification: This gives the public time to think about, deliberate on, and mount a defense against the proposal before it can be enacted.*
* **3.G** A proposal passed in either legislative body expires after 1 year. A proposal that is 80% similar to such an expired proposal may not be voted on by the other body for at least 6 months afterward. 
* **3.H** The council may not vote to initiate any proposal while any proposal passed by the local committee can be voted on by the council. 
  * *Justification: This prevents the council from simply ignoring the local committees.*
* **3.I** The council may not vote on any proposal while any court order of legal clarification is pending waiting for council action that isn't related to that legal clarification order. 
  * *Justification: This prevents the council from simply ignoring the courts when an ambiguity is identified and escalated.*
* **3.J** For the first 5 years of the city, the council can take official actions 6 months out of the year and can propose up to 6,000 words of law, and 6,000 words of city and borough limitations per year. 
  * *Justification: there may be more need of flexibility and touch ups to the law in the beginning as its likely more problems and lessons will come up early on.*

**4. Legislative powers:**

* **4.A** Creating laws defining equivalences of harm for the violation of city laws by a borough's government or its people. Borough governments will be held accountable for all actions by people within the borough that cause harm according to city law, city limitations, or city constitution.
  * *Justification: Holding the borough accountable means the borough itself is incentivized to come up with preventative measures to limit harm done to city property. Or it may decide to shield its residents against the city government if it believes that to be better. The borough can then be expected to prosecute the individuals within their borough as needed.*
* **4.B** Land use designation:
  * Designating a minimal network of travel ways.
  * Creating minimum standards for city-designated travel ways, for the purposes of safety. 
  * Creating visual and informational standards to clearly differentiate travel ways that meet city standards from those that don't (eg borough-designated travel ways where the borough decides not to meet city standards). 
  * Designating certain land areas as protected nature-space.
* **4.C** Courts and police
  * Creating requirements and standards the city's chosen private courts and police must follow.
  * Contracting with or operating a police force that may be no larger than the largest private police force or borough police force in the city. This police force may only be used to enforce rulings by the city court. 
* **4.C** Collecting tax from boroughs. The amount of tax collected must be specified as a single percentage of the tax collected by any given borough. 
* **4.D** Placing limitations, for the purpose of safety and security, on the possession of physical items with at least as much potential for physical harm as gun that fires bullets of a caliber larger than 5mm.

**5. Local Committee Powers**

The following power is passable with a vote through local committees only. Any vote on a proposal that only addresses this subject treats the council vote as passing automatically. 

**5.A** Creating procedures for choosing private courts to handle disputes with the city government, and private police forces to enforce rulings by a city-sanctioned court. 

*Justification: This gives a way to hold the city government to account through a mechanism that isn't entangled with city government officials or bureaucracy, which should eliminate or at least minimize the possibility of a dispute system that unfairly favors the city government.*

### Judicial branch

**6. Courts:**

**6.A** The judicial branch generally consists of private courts selected by the legislature to judge disputes between people of different boroughs or between a person and a borough's government. 

**6.B** Court proceedings on behalf of the city government will be conducted using statutory law. Common law is not used. Precedent may be used in argument, but is not law. Law and contract may be interpreted by any defendant in any way a reasonable person might literally interpret it. Constitutional provisions must be interpreted with the literal meaning intended when the constitutional provision was added to the constitution.  

**7. City court powers:**

* **7.A** Issuing warrants to qualifying citizen applicants for arrest, search, seizure, compulsion of appearing as a witness in court, and compulsion of furnishing evidence in court.
* **7.B** Ordering the legislature to update a law to clarify an ambiguity. 
  * *Justification: This seems far better than allowing courts to create law in cases of ambiguity.*
* **7.C** Determining the jurisdiction in which a dispute will be decided when there is ambiguity in what the jurisdiction for a particular case should be. This may include splitting a case into multiple cases if different parts of the case should be handled in different jurisdictions.
* **7.D** Determining the outcome of disputes between a person and a borough's government, including appealing borough court decisions.
* **7.E** Outcomes ordered by the court may be some or all of the following:
  * Transfer of money or property from the culprit:
    * to the damaged person, or
    * to the party that incurred costs to bring them to justice.
  * Rehabilitation training or therapeutic activity for the culprit.
  * House arrest and/or probation. 
  * Prison time for the culprit, only if A. the person is an active substantial danger to others or B. if they cannot pay a fine high enough to cover the damages and other methods have been shown to be ineffective.
  * Temporary or permanent expulsion from the jurisdiction.
* **7.F** To order an outcome against a person that involves only a transfer of money, rehabilitation, or therapy, there must be clear and convincing evidence, according to the law at the time of the act, that the person was at fault and that the harm done was not lower than estimated.
* **7.G** To order an outcome against a person that involves something other than a transfer of money, rehabilitation training, or therapy, it must be beyond a reasonable doubt that, according to the law at the time of the act, that the person was at fault and that the harm done was not lower than estimated.
  * *Justification: The burden of proof should be greater here because the punishment is more costly to society. While in a transfer of money as payment of damages, the receiver of the damage payment gains as much as the payer loses, the same cannot be said for eg prison time. Therefore we should be more sure of these kinds of punishments.*
* **7.H** The cost of any punishments to the offender may not exceed `(DH + SC)/L + OB + 10% * CF`, where
  * the "cost of any punishments", includes any any court ordered outcomes including prison time, jail time, time arrested or detained, and in the case where no settlement was proposed that carried a lower cost to the defendant than the final outcome, time in court. 
  * `DH` is the Direct Harm done to the victim. This does not include any costs to the victim in bringing suit to the offender. 
  * `SC` is the Societal Cost, the estimated average or marginal cost that this type of harm places on society for each instance.
    * *These are things like the effort and time people must take to be vigilant again harm, preparation to defend themselves, the mental stress involved in that, and opportunity costs for things not done because of the risk of harm from others. The "societal cost" may be included in the estimation of harm done even tho this may sometime or even always result in greater than optimally efficient punishments, because it isn't clear whether or not this is the case. This wiggle room leaves it up to the boroughs to decide what they think is optimal.*
  * `L` is the Likelihood of getting caught with enough evidence to be found guilty. 
    * Things such as incidence (ratio of incidents caught divided by estimated total incidents), intent (negligence vs premeditation), ability to or effort made to avoid getting caught or minimize evidence, level of ability to or effort made to avoid harm in the case of negligence, and the like can be used to justify the level of this likelihood.
    * L must be no lower than 1% - if it would be lower than 1%, the value 1% should be used.
      * *Justification: L is given a minimum value if it is lower than 1% because low-value harm that is extremely unlikely to be caught should not place undue punishment on the few people that are caught. There should be a limit to how much a person should be punished for low-harm actions.*
  * `OB` is the Offender Benefit, any significant measurable benefits the offender receives from the decided punishment (eg rehabilitation care, training, education, etc).
  * `CF` are the Court Fees.
    * *Justification: It might be reasonable to allow courts to charge some of the fee to the defendant to incentivize them not to delay the proceedings.*
  * *Justification: Defendants shouldn't have the cost of legal defense included in their fine because the harm / likelihood of getting caught neutralizes the societal cost such that in the average case, the plaintiff's legal costs should be exceeded by the damages. This does mean that it doesn't make economic sense to prosecute every harm for harms that cost more to prosecute than damages received. This makes sense because it would be inefficient to spend more on prosecution than the cost of the original offense.*
* **7.I** The cost to the state of any punishments of the offender may not exceed `DC * (DH + SH - OG) + OB` where
  * `DH`, `SH`, and `OB` are as above.
  * `DC` (deterred crime) is the cost of deterred harm as a fraction of the total cost of all harm. *For example, if there are 100 crimes of this type in a year and the punishment (if applied to all crimes of this type) would reduce 2 crimes, DC would be 2% (0.02)*
  * `OG` is the offender-gain, an estimated quantification of the value the offender would have gained from their harm-causing actions if not caught.
  * *Justification: For example, it costs money to put people in prison and the benefit is ideally deterrence of future crime. The costs of deterring crime should not exceed the costs of the crime itself. ([see here](http://www.daviddfriedman.com/Academic/Becker_Chapter/Becker_Chapter.html)).* 
* **7.J** Any ordered transfer of money to a party is given as collection right to the recipient or recipients, who can then organize or sell that right as they see fit. 
* **7.K** Any outcome that is not money-transfer is organized by the city executive branch. 
* **7.L** A court has the discretion to dismiss a case where the harm estimated is below the court fees.
  * *Justification: The legal code is set up so that small harms can be pursued but are not monetarily beneficial to pursue. This provision allows the court to prevent someone willing to eat the cost from harassing someone else by obtaining disruptive court orders.* 

Additional justifications: *Damage done is the obvious metric by which to decide remuneration of the victim. Dividing this by incidents_caught/estimated_incidence means that types of harm that are caught less often can have a higher punitive fine or sentence to compensate for the fact that the type of harm often goes without restitution. Fining a person more than this would be unfair and not economically justifiable. There are some circumstances where doing harm might be justified if the result is more good is done than harm, however the harm must still be paid for and if harm is unfairly punished, it would cause people to do less good in those cases. Over-punishing crime is a sign that the goal is subjugation and not justice.*

*It might be ideal that the fine+sentence always be set to this maximum, since it gives appropriate incentives to private prosecutors to prosecute rarely caught incidents of relatively low-cost harm. The exception might be situations where a permit is requested and steps are taken to assure that the vast majority of instance of harm are recorded so that required compensation can be tallied, in which case only the harm done should be compensated for without any punitive damages. This is a related paper with a similar conclusion: https://www.nber.org/system/files/chapters/c3625/c3625.pdf.*

**8. Judicial consortium:** 

**8.A** When there is a dispute between anyone and officials or actions of the city government, the case must first be decided in a borough court. If the decision convicts a city government official and they appeal, a judicial consortium is formed. A judge is chosen by each borough to make up the judicial consortium. The consortium then presides and decides the dispute by either validating the decision made, dismissing it, or amending it. 

### 9. Executive branch

**9.A** An executive branch made up of a Mayor who organizes the implementation of laws. The mayor is elected in a score vote by the local committees, where the total score for a candidate is found by multiplying the value-weighted average score by the population-weighted average score for that candidate. 

* *Justification: multiplying the scores using both weightings strongly favors non-polarizing candidates.*

**9.B** The Mayor may also organize investigations or lawsuits against those who cause damages to the city government's property. 

## 10. Borough powers

A borough may not use powers that aren't explicitly enumerated in this constitution. Borough's can use the following powers:

* **10.A** Creating laws defining property rights, contract law, equivalences of harm (ie assigning a monetary value to a particular amount of a type of harm), and dispute resolution requirements.
  * **10.A.1** Harm must be actual harm done or risk imposition. In the case of risk imposition, this must be a concrete physical state or condition created by the person who allegedly caused harm by risk imposition that increases the probability of manifest harm, and may not be based on that person's estimated mental state.
    * Harm can include exposure to elevated risk (eg negligence) in lieu of manifest harm because such situations transfer a cost of risk onto other as a negative externality.
* **10.B** Creating laws defining guardianship rights and responsibilities. 
* **10.C** Creating laws that promote the existence of multiple independent competitors in a market.
* **10.D** Subsidizing a second competitor in the markets of court services, police, temporary holding facilities, or prison facilities, only if that second competitor is not the market leader by number of customers nor revenue nor profit and there is no third competitor that meets enacted requirements.  
* City council:
  * **10.E** Creating constraints on council member eligibility, term length, and selection process. The selection process must be proportional vote where the number of voters who voted in each candidate is known.
* Tax and externalities:
  * **10.F** Creating laws for the purpose of correcting positive and negative externalities, including the collection of a land-value tax or land externality tax.
  * **10.G** Collecting income tax, only if collecting a land-value tax of 95% of the land's external value is not sufficient to fund the borough's government.
  * **10.H** Reimbursing taxes from levels of government above the city level in the form of direct payments or in the form of tax reductions. 
    * *Justification: Reimbursing state and federal taxes can reduce deadweight losses without costing the borough much or possibly anything.*
* Courts
  * **10.I** Creating requirements and standards for the borough court system.
  * **10.J** Issuing warrants for arrest, search, or seizure.
  * **10.K** Granting a plaintiff the right to pursue a case in a court at a higher-level than the city, only when remedies ordered within city or borough law have become infeasible to enforce within the city.
* Police
  * **10.L** Creating procedures to choose private police forces to enforce rulings by a borough sanctioned court. 
  * **10.M** Creating a procedure and requirements to allow people or entities to place surveillance equipment on public ways or other public property. Such equipment must be placed for a particular purpose or purposes, and only used for those granted purposes. The purpose may only be for gathering evidence of an externality (eg noise cameras or surveillance of any kind of community harm). 
* Emergencies
  * **10.N** Creating procedures to choose private groups to hire for the alleviation of emergencies. 
  * **10.O** Borrowing money for the purposes of alleviating an emergency that affects more than 500 people. The city may not borrow more than 20% of its yearly tax revenue, and any borrowed money must be paid back within 4 years.
* **10.P** Creating a welfare program in the form of a universal basic income. The total of yearly payouts may not exceed 10% of the government's yearly tax revenue and also may not exceed 0.1% of the total GDP of the borough.
  * *Justification: When the tax money for welfare comes from a land value tax, the tapering of a negative income tax has [effects similar to a tax](https://micahe.substack.com/p/means-testing-is-an-inefficient-tax?r=1fcmli)* *which causes deadweight losses. UBI does not have this problem when funded with LVT (tho it would if it were funded with other taxes).*
  * *Justification: Only the borough is allowed to have a welfare program and not the city, so that the affects of that program can be compared to adjacent boroughs.*
* Land use
  * **10.Q** Creating zones that have differentiated laws related to externalities.
  * **10.R** Designating additional travel ways beyond city-designated ways.
  * **10.S** Creating additional minimum standards for city and borough travel ways and utilities within the borough. 
  * **10.T** Designating certain land as protected nature-space or park space.
* **10.U** Creating requirements and procedures for visitation, immigration, and expulsion. No person may be prevented from leaving a borough or the city except as is required for being lawfully detained for suspected harm or imprisoned for harm determined by a court. Any person with the ability to move freely within a particular borough may move freely throughout the entire city unless explicitly and individually expelled from a borough through due process of law within that borough. 
* Licensure and information requirements
  * **10.V** Creating licensure requirements for doing certain activity likely to cause negative externalities, including but not limited to dog ownership, operation of heavy equipment that weighs more than 300 pounds or can perform more than 10 kilowatts of work, handling hazardous materials, demolition work, construction, and policing. 
  * **10.W** Creating licensure requirements for doing certain activity likely to be engineered to trigger natural mechanisms in the brain and body in unnatural ways known to lead to significantly harmful effects to the person doing the activity, and creating labeling or other information disclosures requirements for those providing those activities as a product or service, including but not limited to gambling, drugs, porn, use of social media, and processed or engineered foods. 
  * **10.X** Whenever any licenses are required, the government must ensure there is a training option that costs no more than 2 times the median daily income of a city resident and is expected to take no more than 30 hours to complete. If such an option does not exist at any time, people are free to perform the relevant actions without a license for the period of time between when the required option ceased to be available until 30 days after a required option becomes available again. 
  * **10.Y** A borough may prohibit people from assisting a ward in performing any activity that requires a license without approval of their guardian, or selling a ward a service that requires certain information disclosure without approval of their guardian.

## 11. Constitutional city and borough limitations

* **11.A.1** A preponderance of evidence must show that a law is expected to benefit society more than it costs society before it can be enacted into law.
* **11.A.2** Violations of the constitution and attempted violations will be treated as serious crimes and will have serious consequences. A constitutional offense is defined as any decision or supporting vote by an official of or acting under the direct or indirect direction of a city or borough government for a law, decision, or action that is later determined to violate the constitution by a judicial consortium. A convicted person is suspended from office or position and the action is considered damage equivalent to between 1-20% of their wealth. 
* **11.A.3** Property rights or other laws defined at higher levels of government than the city can not be enforced by city, borough, or private courts selected as part of this constitution unless the relevant legislature or contract specifically opts into the enforcement of the relevant specific higher-level law. By default, otherwise enforcement of those laws is left to state and federal police and courts and all claims arising under Texas, Federal, or County law are expressly excluded and split from the claim. People agree to waive a defense of Res Judicata in cases where this wavier of higher-level rights is unenforceable and the plaintiff decides to also bring the case concerning claims separate from those decided in courts empowered by this constitution (eg claims arising from state/federal law). If a dispute involves both Borough and higher level claims, the parties agree to joint motion to stay the case until the higher-level court determines the statutory issues.
  * *Justification: This isolates the city from state and federal power and laws, which may work very poorly in an environment where laws are enforced with full economic efficiency.*
  * *Justification:  The provisions waiving Res Judicata and an agreement to stay the case when there are claims comingled with higher level law are to avoid collateral estoppel and voiding of city/borough court decisions when they must leave higher level law for higher level courts and make court decisions without considering those higher level laws.*
* **11.A.4** Laws passed in a borough around negative externalities (including intrusion of matter and energy such as sound, light, smells, etc) will be respected by those at the border in a different borough. 
  * For example, if a person in borough A creates enough noise to violate borough B's noise ordinance within the borders of borough B, that person can be prosecuted and if brought to city court that prosecution must be upheld. 
  * *Justification: Violating property rights should be corrected regardless of where the violator was when they acted in a violating manner.*
* **11.A.5** No law that requires, incentivizes, or disincentivizes particular methods or processes for private citizens can be enacted. All laws that make requirements on private citizens must define the requirement in terms of outcomes, qualities, and metrics.
* **11.A.6** No borough can grow larger than 300 acres nor can any part of a borough be more than 6 miles from any other part of the borough.
  * *Justification: This keeps jurisdictions small and compact, making it easy to vote with your feet. The compactness requirement also limits potential extractive behavior (eg a long thin borough that eliminates LVT to extract land value from across the border).*

**Government limitations expressed as rights and freedoms of private persons:**

A "right" in this section means that a city or borough government cannot put any barriers (eg requirements, fees, fines), incentives (eg payment or grants of privileges), or other consequences in place for someone to engage in that right, not engage in that right, or engage in that right in a particular way, excepting the constitutional powers of requiring licensure, information disclosure, and the related limitations regarding wards listed elsewhere in this document. 

* **11.B.1** Right to consensually interact, contract, and act in any way that harms no other person. 
* **11.B.2** Right to contract with covenants. A covenant is only valid as long as there is at all times some entity with the contractual power to alter or remove the covenant. 
* **11.B.3** Freedom of thought, expression, religion, and assembly in ways that do not cause direct and immediate harm to another.
* **11.B.4** Right to sue, including the right to sue officials and agencies of the city and boroughs for breach of constitution, law, or private contract that person is party to. The government does not have sovereign immunity and cannot grant immunity from suit to anyone. 
* **11.B.5** Freedom to be assumed innocent until proven guilty. One must never have to legally defend oneself until the party bringing up charges or suit has been determined to have a case that would not end in favor of the accused without legal defense.
* **11.B.6** Right to secede from one borough and create a new borough or join and adjacent borough as a group. This requires a contiguous block of land containing at least 50 adult residents and at least 25 acres and requires approval by a land-value weighted 75% vote by the land owners as well as a land-area weighted 75% vote by the land owners. Any successful secession vote will take affect in a timeframe to be specified by city law, no sooner than 4 months after the successful vote.
  * *Justification: This vote is done by land-value and land-area but not residency because the jurisdiction that a property is in should be the sole purview of the land owner, not any renter.*
* Dispute rights
  * Right to choose private law. 
    * **11.B.7** Any person or entity can register a code of law, code of law scoring list, private court company, or court scoring list with the city for a fee to be set by city law. 
      * Each registered code of law can specify a fee for the use of the code of law that will be paid by the plaintiff. Any code of law that derives from another code of law must specify which sections derive from which code of law and obtain the right to use those sections from their respective owners.
      * Each registered code of law scoring list will manage a dynamically updated list of scored codes of law with a specified fee for the use of the list.
      * Each registered court company specifies their compensation policy for use of the court.
      * Each registered court scoring list will manage a dynamically updated list of scored courts with a specified fee for the use of the list.
      * These companies must publish any change in pricing/compensation policy at least 1 month before changing that policy. If used, the fee/compensation paid will be what was registered at the time the plaintiff registered their current list.
        * *This is to ensure that there's time to update lists that people make decisions based on.*
    * **11.B.8** Any person or entity can then register a scored list of private codes of law and code of law scoring companies with the city, and in any dispute, if both parties in the dispute both approved of at least one code of law (directly or through a code of law scoring list), one of their approved codes of law will be used to resolve the dispute in place of city and borough law. 
      * Scores will be 0 to 100, expected in order from highest score to lowest score. Items with the same score but higher on the list will be treated as higher priority than ones lower on the list. 
      * When listing a code of law scoring list, the person or entity registers a maximum fee to pay and the minimum score (according to the scoring list) for the underlying legal code. The same code of law scoring list can be listed (and scored) multiple times with different limits for fee and score of the underlying legal codes.
      * If both parties have approved multiple matching codes of law, the code of law with the lowest sum of the scores given by the plaintiff and defendant will be chosen. 
      * If there is still a tie, the highest priority code of law for the defendant out of the remaining options is chosen. 
      * If the parties have not both opted into the same code of law, the code of law that the borough has opted into is used.
      * If neither party has opted into the same code of law but both have opted into some alternative code of law, the harm done found by a court may not be larger than the higher amount of harm done as specified by the top choice of code of law for each party. 
    * **11.B.9** Any person or entity can register a scored list of court companies and court scoring lists. When a dispute is brought, a court company will be chosen in the same way as a legal code is chosen based on the person's or entity's registered list. If the parties in question don't have any overlapping court company choices, the borough's chosen default court is used.
  * **11.B.10** Right to due process. Including freedom from double jeopardy, attainder, ex post facto prosecution, being compelled to be a witness against oneself, and excessive bail. Also including the right to a public and speedy trial with impartial judges.
  * **11.B.11** Right to a court decision by an impartial jury of those local to the crime in any case where non-monetary transfer outcomes are possible or where the harm done is claimed to have a monetary equivalent of more than 10% of the wealth of the accused or more than $10,000 (2024$).
  * **11.B.12** Right to choice in dispute resolution. 
    * When defaulting to city or borough decided court, at least 3 choices of court companies must be offered to the accused. The court companies offered must have no overlap in direct or indirect ownership.

### 12. Limitations on all persons

These are limitations on all people within the borders of the city, regardless of legal status or any other status.

* Use of force
  * **12.A** Use of force may only be used when the exercise of persuasion, advice, and warning is found to be insufficient to protect the rights of oneself or another. When force is used, a force may be used that is no greater than the minimum degree of physical force a reasonable person would think is necessary to protect the rights of oneself or another.
* Policing
  * Detainment
    * **12.B** No person or property may be detained without reasonable suspicion of the active violation of someone else's rights and being informed of such.
    * **12.C** No person or property may be detained for longer than reasonable suspicion exists nor longer than it should take to determine whether probable cause exists. 
  * Search
    * **12.D** Forcible search of items on a person may not be done without the person being lawfully detained or arrested.
    * **12.E** No property may be forcibly searched without obtaining and producing a court warrant establishing and recording probable cause, the place to be searched, what is expected to be found, and reasonable suspicion that items will be found that are not legally possessed, evidence of a violation, or are weapons. 
  * Seizure 
    * **12.F** No property, other than weapons of someone detained or arrested, may be seized without obtaining and producing a court warrant establishing and recording probable cause and the items to be seized. Property may not be seized without probable cause that either the property is not legally possessed or that seizure is necessary as evidence for the relevant case.
  * Arrests
    * **12.G** An arrest is only permitted if the estimated flight risk is great enough when evaluated against the estimated damage done, or if risk of repeat offense is estimated to be high, and the person to be arrested is informed of these details. 
  * Holding at a holding facility
    * **12.H** No person or property other than weapons for which the validity of the arrest or seizure has not been affirmed may be forcibly held longer than it should reasonably take for the appropriate court to affirm or disaffirmed that validity. No weapons seized without affirmation of a probable cause may be held longer than the owner. 
    * **12.I** No person or property may be forcibly held for longer than it should take for a trial to begun for their case or for longer than their court ordered sentence. 
    * **12.J** When it is necessary to bring a person to a temporary holding facility or prison, at least 3 choices of independent companies must be offered. Each of these companies must not have overlap in direct nor indirect ownership. If the person to be held has ownership in one of the options, that person is only offered the 2 options they do not have ownership in.
    * **12.K** Any person forcibly held keeps all their rights except the right to leave the designated holding area. 
    * **12.L** Any person forcibly held must have adequate regular access to communicate with the outside world. 
    * **12.M** Holding facilities may not forbid recording (eg of video or audio) by any person at the facility.
    * **12.N** Any person forcibly held for longer than 10 hours must be given a private, clean, secure sleeping place with a door that they can lock. 
  * Compensation
    * **12.O** A person detained, forcible searched, subject to seizure, arrested, or forcibly held, regardless of the outcome of any related court case, is owed compensation proportional to the time and level of inconvenience by the party that inconvenienced them.  This compensation may include a reduction in remedies that burden the person in question.
* **12.P** To the fullest extent that law of higher-level governments allows, those within the city must agree to not sue or press charges against anyone in a court of any higher-level government for events that affected you within the borders of the freestead by a person or entity under the jurisdiction of the freestead except as described by the freestead’s legal codes.
